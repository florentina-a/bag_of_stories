In times past there lived a king and queen, who said to each other every day of their lives, "Would that we had a child!" and yet they had none. But it happened once that when the queen was bathing, there came a frog out of the water, and he squatted on the ground, and said to her,

"Thy wish shall be fulfilled; before a year has gone by, thou shalt bring a daughter into the world." And so it came to pass, that the queen was delivered of a beautiful girl-child, whom they called Briar-Rose. They rejoiced greatly, and the king said,

"She shall live in the finest possible style; there shall not be wanting to her the costliest garments, nor the richest jewels."

One day when Briar-Rose was fifteen years old, the whole court was celebrating her birthday. The palace was gay with flags and banners, and every room was filled with roses, the favorite flower of the princess. But there was one room, the labor- chamber, which was shut up, and no one knew what was there.

There, fast asleep on a bed of roses, lay the fair Briar-Rose. The evil fairy Carabosse, who had not been invited to the festivities, was filled with rage. She had sworn that the beautiful princess should never have the happiness of growing up. She had cast a spell upon the princess, that she should prick her finger on the spindle of a spinning wheel, and fall into a deep sleep, from which there was no waking.

The wicked fairy was not idle. She transformed herself into an old hag and went into the palace, seeking the labor-room. Finding the door ajar, she crept in, and there, in the dim light, she saw the sleeping Briar-Rose.

With a gleam of malicious pleasure in her eyes, she took up a spindle, which was lying there, and holding it above the princess, she whispered,

"Prick, prick, O lovely Briar-Rose, prick, prick, with the spindle of doom!"

As she spoke the words, the sharp point of the spindle touched the princess's finger, and in an instant she was enveloped in a deep sleep.

The courtiers, who had been waiting for the princess, were at last disturbed by a sound from the labor-room. They hurried thither, and found the beautiful Briar-Rose asleep on the bed of roses. A deep sleep, from which there was no waking.

The king and queen were inconsolable with grief, but they were not idle. They sent out searchers to find the wicked fairy Carabosse, and to seek a remedy for the spell that had been cast upon their daughter. They searched in far-off lands, but in vain.

At last, a good fairy, who had not been invited to the birthday festivities, came to their aid. She had heard of the misfortune that had befallen the princess, and she knew how to break the spell. She took the princess to a secluded castle deep in the forest, where she lived in peace, surrounded by enchanters and magicians.

There, the princess, Briar-Rose, slept on, in a deep, dreamless sleep, until one day, a hundred years had passed, and a handsome prince, who had heard of the sleeping beauty, came to seek her hand in marriage. He found her in the castle, and when he kissed her, she awoke, and they were married, and they lived happily ever after.
