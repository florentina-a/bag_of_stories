There was once a miller who was poor, but he had one beautiful daughter. It happened one day that he came to speak with the king, and, to give himself consequence, he told him that he had a daughter who could spin gold out of straw. The king said to the miller: "That is an art that pleases me well; if thy daughter is as clever as you say, bring her to my castle to-morrow, that I may put her to the proof."

The next morning, the miller brought his daughter to the castle. She was a modest girl, with eyes like the morning sky and hands calloused from honest work. The king led her into a chamber filled with straw, handed her a spinning wheel, and said, “Set to work at once. If by morning you have not spun this straw into gold, you shall die.” Then he locked the door and left her alone.

The poor girl sat down and wept, for she had no idea how to spin straw into gold. As the moon rose high in the sky, a strange little man appeared in the room. He wore a coat of many colors and had a beard that brushed the floor. “Why do you weep so bitterly?” he asked in a voice like wind rustling through dry leaves.

“I must spin this straw into gold, and I do not know how,” she sobbed. The little man tilted his head and smiled. “What will you give me if I do it for you?” he asked. The girl, desperate, offered him her necklace. He took it, sat at the wheel, and spun all the straw into gold before dawn.

When the king returned and saw the gold, he was astonished and delighted—but his greed was not yet satisfied. He led the girl to a larger room filled with even more straw and gave her the same command. Again, she was left alone, and again the little man appeared. This time, she gave him her ring, and he spun the straw into gold by morning.

Still not content, the king brought her to a third room, larger than the first two combined, and promised that if she succeeded once more, he would make her his queen. That night, when the little man appeared, the girl had nothing left to give. “Then promise me,” he said, “your firstborn child when you are queen.” In her fear and desperation, she agreed.

The little man spun the straw into gold once more, and in the morning, the king, true to his word, married the miller’s daughter. She became queen, and for a time, all was well. A year later, she gave birth to a beautiful child and had nearly forgotten the strange little man—until he appeared in her chamber, demanding his payment.

The queen was horrified and begged him to let her keep her child. The little man, moved by her tears, offered her a chance: “If you can guess my name within three days, you may keep your child.” She agreed, and he vanished into the shadows.

All that day and the next, the queen sent messengers across the land to gather every name they could find. She guessed names from the common to the obscure—Caspar, Melchior, Balthazar—but each time the little man returned, he only laughed and said, “That is not my name.”

On the third day, one of her messengers returned with a strange tale. Deep in the forest, he had seen a little man dancing around a fire, singing, “Tonight, tonight, my plans I make, tomorrow, tomorrow, the baby I take. The queen will never win the game, for Rumpelstiltskin is my name!”

That evening, when the little man returned, the queen smiled and asked, “Is your name Conrad?” “No.” “Is it Heinrich?” “No.” Then she said, “Is it Rumpelstiltskin?” The little man shrieked in fury, stamped his foot so hard it sank into the floor, and vanished in a puff of smoke, never to be seen again.
