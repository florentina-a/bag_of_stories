There was once a miller who was poor, but he had one beautiful daughter. It happened one day that he came to speak with the king, and, to give himself consequence, he told him that he had a daughter who could spin gold out of straw. The king said to the miller: "That is an art that pleases me well; if thy daughter is as clever as you say, bring her to my castle to-morrow, that I may put her to the proof."

And so, with a mixture of fear and pride, the miller returned home and delivered the king's summons to his daughter. Her eyes widened in terror. "But father!" she cried, "I know nothing of spinning gold from straw! You've boasted us into a terrible predicament!" The miller, however, was too caught up in the potential for royal favor to truly grasp the gravity of his lie. He simply patted her shoulder and assured her that something would surely come to them.

The next morning, the beautiful daughter, whose name was Lena, was led to the castle. The king, a stern man with an expectant gaze, brought her to a chamber filled from floor to ceiling with straw. A spinning wheel stood in the center, glinting ominously in the morning light. "Now," he commanded, "set to work. By morning, if you have not spun this straw into gold, you shall surely die." With that, he locked the door, leaving Lena alone in the golden-hued prison.

Lena sank to the floor, despair washing over her. Tears streamed down her face as she contemplated her impossible task. How could she, a simple girl who had only ever spun humble flax, turn worthless straw into gleaming gold? The hours ticked by, each one amplifying her terror. The room grew darker as evening approached, and still, the straw remained stubbornly, mockingly, straw.

Just as she had given up all hope, a small, grotesque figure with a pointed red cap and a long, scraggly beard suddenly appeared in the middle of the room. Lena gasped, startled, but the creature merely grinned, a wide, unsettling smile that revealed too many teeth. "Good evening, fair maiden," it croaked, its voice like dry leaves skittering across cobblestones. "Why do you weep so bitterly?"

Lena, though still frightened, found herself compelled to explain her predicament. "I must spin this straw into gold by morning, or the king will have my life," she sobbed, gesturing helplessly at the mountainous pile. The creature listened intently, its beady eyes never leaving her face. When she finished, it chuckled, a dry, rattling sound. "I can help you with that," it said, "for a price."

"What do you want?" Lena asked, her voice trembling with a flicker of desperate hope. The impish figure circled the spinning wheel, its gaze fixed on a delicate necklace Lena wore, a simple silver locket containing a lock of her mother's hair. "Give me that trinket around your neck," it demanded, pointing a long, bony finger. Without hesitation, Lena unclasped the locket. What was a keepsake compared to her life?

The creature snatched the locket with surprising speed and then, with a flick of its wrist, sat down at the spinning wheel. It began to spin with an unnatural fervor. Whirring and whizzing, the wheel spun so quickly that Lena could barely follow its movements. And as it spun, a miraculous transformation occurred. Golden threads began to emerge from the straw, coiling onto the bobbin with an almost magical glow.

By the time the first rays of dawn peeked through the high windows, the entire mountain of straw had been transformed into shimmering piles of pure gold thread. The imp stood up, stretched, and with another unsettling grin, vanished as suddenly as it had appeared, leaving behind only the scent of straw and a faint, metallic tang of gold.

When the king arrived, his eyes nearly popped out of his head. The sight of the gleaming gold filled him with avarice. "This is truly remarkable!" he exclaimed, a covetous glint in his eye. "But you can do more, can't you?" He led her to an even larger chamber, this one overflowing with an even greater quantity of straw. "Spin this into gold by morning," he commanded, "and you shall be greatly rewarded."

Lena, though weary, felt a surge of grim determination. She knew the imp would return, and she knew it would demand an even higher price. As the king locked the door once more, leaving her alone with the daunting task, she wondered what precious possession she would have to relinquish this time to save her life.
