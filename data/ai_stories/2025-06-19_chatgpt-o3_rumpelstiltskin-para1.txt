There was once a miller who was poor, but he had one beautiful daughter. It happened one day that he came to speak with the king, and, to give himself consequence, he told him that he had a daughter who could spin gold out of straw. The king said to the miller: "That is an art that pleases me well; if thy daughter is as clever as you say, bring her to my castle to-morrow, that I may put her to the proof."

The miller’s daughter was fetched to the castle at dawn, her slippers still damp with dew from the meadow path. Servants led her through echoing corridors until she stood before an oaken door banded with iron. The king himself unlatched it, revealing a small chamber piled high with straw and a single tarnished spinning-wheel at its center. “By sunrise,” he said, voice cool as winter sleet, “all this must be gold or your life is forfeit.” The door slammed and the key turned, leaving her with only the rattle of mice in the rafters for company.

Tears blurred her sight as she fingered the brittle straw, each stalk mocking her helplessness. Midnight’s first bell was still humming when a draught stirred the torchflame and a queer little man no taller than her waist stepped from the shadows. His coat was forest-green and stitched with fallen leaves; his eyes shone like jet beads. “Why the waterworks, maiden?” he chirped, tipping a cap that resembled a curled hazelnut husk.

She told him the king’s demand. The little man’s lips puckered in a thoughtful whistle. “An easy task—for a price. What will you give?” Around her neck she wore a silver locket, a keepsake of her mother. Trembling, she unclasped it. The stranger snatched the trinket, hopped to the wheel and, humming a tune older than oak trees, began to spin. Straw whispered, spindle blurred, and coils of shining gold gathered like sunlit rivers at his feet.

By dawn the room glittered. The king entered, delight flaring in his greedy gaze. Yet marvel was swiftly devoured by appetite; he ordered her to a larger chamber thrice the size, brimmed with straw to the ceiling like a granary of dry thunder. “Do it again,” he commanded, “and perhaps you shall live.”

That night the little man returned, finding her knuckles white on the spinning-wheel. “Payment,” he piped, twirling on one heel. She slipped a ring—her last heirloom—off her finger. His grin widened, and once more the straw surrendered, transforming into reels of gold so radiant that dawn’s light seemed pale in comparison.

The king, half-mad with avarice, escorted her to the great hall: a vast vault of straw enough to fill ten barns. “Spin this—and I will take you for my queen,” he said, for what better way to chain wealth than to wed it? When the stranger appeared on the third night, she had nothing left to give. “Then promise me,” he whispered, voice soft as burrowing roots, “your first-born child.” Despair clawed her heart, yet the thought of another sunrise without life forced her to nod.

Golden thread sang beneath the rafters till morning. True to his word, the king married her that very day, and jubilation drowned the memory of bargains struck in darkness. A year later a prince was born, pink-cheeked and bright-eyed. Festivities rang through every village, but when the last lantern guttered, the little man stepped into the nursery, cloaked now in autumn shadows. “I have come for what is mine.”

Terror returned in a rush. The queen fell to her knees, pleading for mercy. He relented—slightly. “Three nights I’ll grant. Guess my name, and I’ll vanish forever. Fail, and the child is mine.” With that, he vanished like candle smoke. Messengers galloped in every direction, scouring the realm for odd names: Humplethatch, Twillikin, Cobblecrook—none were right.

On the third evening, a weary rider brought strange news. Deep in the forest he had spied a tiny cottage where a creature danced around a fire, crowing,  
“Tonight I bake, tomorrow I brew,  
The next I’ll claim a child I’m due.  
No one knows this game I win—  
For Rumpelstiltskin is my name within!”

Armed with that secret, the queen waited. The little man burst in, smug and sure. She toyed with false guesses—“Melchior? Thistlebeard?” His grin grew sharper. At last she spoke softly, “Could it be… Rumpelstiltskin?” The name struck him like a hammer on glass. He shrieked, stomped his foot so hard the stone cracked, and he vanished in a whirlwind of sparks and bitter smoke, never to trouble the kingdom again. And thus the miller’s daughter, by wit and a whispered name, kept both her child and her crown.
